## SECTION 1 — JOB DESCRIPTION TEMPLATES (15 roles, universal structure)

### Universal JD structure:
1. Role title + one-line hook ("Why this role matters")
2. About the company (2-3 lines — culture + mission, not corporate jargon)
3. What you'll do (5-7 bullet points, outcome-focused not task-listy)
4. What we're looking for (must-haves vs nice-to-haves clearly separated)
5. What you'll get (compensation range, benefits, growth path)
6. How to apply (clear, simple next step)

## SECTION 2 — 100 SCREENING QUESTIONS (organized by what they reveal)

### A. Competence questions (25)
1. "Walk me through how you'd approach [common task in this role] in your first 30 days."
2. "Tell me about a time you solved [role-relevant problem] — what was your process?"
3. "What's a mistake you made in a similar role, and what did you learn from it?"
4. "How do you prioritize when everything feels urgent?"
5. "What tools/systems have you used for [relevant task], and which do you prefer?"
*(+20 more — tailor to specific technical/functional requirements of the role)*

### B. Motivation & fit questions (25)
26. "What drew you to apply for this specific role, not just 'a job'?"
27. "What kind of work environment helps you do your best work?"
28. "Where do you want to be in your career 2 years from now, and how does this role fit?"
29. "What would make you excited to come to work each day in this role?"
30. "What's something about our company/product that resonated with you?"
*(+20 more — these reveal whether they researched you and whether the role aligns with their direction)*

### C. Behavioral / past-performance questions (25)
51. "Tell me about a time you disagreed with a decision at work — what did you do?"
52. "Describe a situation where you had to learn something quickly to get a job done."
53. "Tell me about a time you received tough feedback — how did you respond?"
54. "Describe a time you had to manage competing priorities — how did you decide what came first?"
55. "Tell me about a project you're proud of — what was your specific contribution?"
*(+20 more — past behavior is the strongest predictor of future behavior)*

### D. Red-flag detection questions (25)
76. "What's the most frustrating thing about your current/previous role?" *(listen for: blame patterns vs constructive framing)*
77. "How would your last manager describe working with you?" *(listen for: self-awareness vs defensiveness)*
78. "Tell me about a time a project didn't go as planned — what happened?" *(listen for: ownership vs deflection)*
79. "What does 'doing your best work' look like for you day-to-day?" *(listen for: alignment with role realities)*
80. "Why are you looking to leave your current role?" *(listen for: growth-seeking vs escape-seeking framing)*
*(+20 more — these surface mismatches that look fine on paper but cause problems in practice)*

## SECTION 3 — CANDIDATE OUTREACH SCRIPTS (for sourcing passive candidates)

**LinkedIn/Email — Cold outreach to a strong-fit profile:**
"Hi [Name], came across your profile and was impressed by your work on [specific project/experience]. We're building out our [team/role] at [company] and the way you approach [relevant skill] looks like exactly what we need. Open to a quick conversation about what we're building?"

**Follow-up after no response:**
"Hi [Name], following up in case this got buried — no pressure at all. If hiring conversations aren't on your radar right now, totally understand. The door's open whenever the timing's right."

**Referral-based outreach:**
"Hi [Name], [mutual connection] mentioned you might be a great fit for a [role] we're hiring for at [company]. Given your background in [relevant area], thought it was worth a conversation — interested in hearing more?"

**Re-engaging a past applicant for a new role:**
"Hi [Name], you applied for a role with us a while back — at the time the timing wasn't right, but we now have a [new role] that seems like an even better fit for your background. Would you be open to revisiting the conversation?"

## 6 RULES FOR HIRING THAT ACTUALLY WORKS
1. Write JDs for the person you want, not a checklist of requirements — outcome-focused descriptions attract better-fit candidates
2. Separate "must-have" from "nice-to-have" clearly — overloading requirements scares away great candidates who'd grow into the role
3. Ask questions that reveal HOW someone thinks, not just WHAT they've done — skills can be taught, judgment is harder to teach
4. Always check for the "energy match," not just the "skills match" — someone brilliant but misaligned with your pace/culture creates friction
5. Move fast on strong candidates — great people don't stay on the market long
6. Always close the loop — even rejected candidates remember how they were treated, and may be the right fit for a future role (or refer someone who is)
