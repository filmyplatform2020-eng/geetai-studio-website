## UNIVERSAL CONTRACT STRUCTURE
1. Parties — full legal names, addresses, effective date
2. Scope of work/agreement — what's being exchanged, in detail
3. Payment terms — amount, schedule, method, late-payment terms
4. Timeline/duration — start date, milestones, end/renewal terms
5. Confidentiality — what's protected, for how long
6. IP/ownership — who owns what's created during the engagement
7. Termination — how either party can exit, notice period, what happens to payments/deliverables
8. Dispute resolution — mediation/arbitration preference, governing jurisdiction
9. Signatures — both parties, date, witness if needed

## TEMPLATE 1 — SERVICE AGREEMENT (GENERAL)
**Use for:** Any service-based engagement — consulting, agency work, freelance projects
**Key clauses to customize:** Scope definition (be SPECIFIC — vague scope = scope creep disputes), payment milestones, revision limits

## TEMPLATE 2 — RETAINER AGREEMENT
**Use for:** Ongoing monthly engagements (marketing retainers, advisory, maintenance)
**Key clauses to customize:** Monthly scope cap (hours/deliverables), rollover policy, renewal/cancellation notice period
**Protects you from:** Scope expanding silently month-over-month without renegotiation

## TEMPLATE 3 — NDA (NON-DISCLOSURE AGREEMENT)
**Use for:** Sharing sensitive info before/during a deal — pitches, partnerships, hiring
**Key clauses to customize:** Definition of "confidential information," duration of obligation (typically 1-3 years), permitted exceptions (legally required disclosure)
**Protects you from:** Ideas/data being shared or used without permission

## TEMPLATE 4 — FREELANCE/CONTRACTOR AGREEMENT
**Use for:** Hiring independent contractors/freelancers for project work
**Key clauses to customize:** Independent-contractor status clause (avoids employment misclassification), deliverable specifications, IP transfer terms
**Protects you from:** Misclassification issues, unclear deliverable ownership

## TEMPLATE 5 — PARTNERSHIP/COLLABORATION AGREEMENT
**Use for:** Joint ventures, co-marketing deals, revenue-share collaborations
**Key clauses to customize:** Revenue/profit split formula, decision-making authority, exit terms if one party wants out
**Protects you from:** Ambiguity over "who owns what" and "who decides what" once the partnership is running

## TEMPLATE 6 — CLIENT ONBOARDING AGREEMENT
**Use for:** Setting expectations with new clients from day one
**Key clauses to customize:** Communication expectations (response times, channels), what's included vs billed-separately, cancellation/refund policy
**Protects you from:** "I thought that was included" disputes down the line

## TEMPLATE 7 — VENDOR/SUPPLIER AGREEMENT
**Use for:** Recurring purchases from suppliers/vendors
**Key clauses to customize:** Quality standards and rejection terms, delivery timelines and penalties for delay, pricing-revision notice period

**Use for:** Hiring team members (full-time/part-time)

## TEMPLATE 9 — LICENSING AGREEMENT
**Use for:** Licensing your product/content/brand for others to use

**Use for:** Formally closing out an engagement — especially ones with disputes or early exits

## PLAIN-LANGUAGE CLAUSE EXPLAINER (use this to explain terms to clients who aren't legally fluent)
- **"Indemnification"** = "If something goes wrong because of your actions, you cover the resulting costs — and vice versa"
- **"Force majeure"** = "Neither party is held responsible for delays caused by events outside their control (natural disasters, government action, etc.)"
- **"Severability"** = "If one clause is found invalid, the rest of the agreement still stands"
- **"Governing law"** = "Which region's laws apply if there's ever a dispute"
- **"Assignment"** = "Whether either party can transfer their rights/obligations to someone else"

## 6 RULES FOR CONTRACTS THAT PREVENT DISPUTES
1. Be specific in scope — "marketing services" invites disputes; "4 social posts/week + 1 monthly report" doesn't
2. Always define what happens if either party wants to exit early
3. Put payment triggers in writing — tie payments to specific milestones, not vague timeframes
4. Address IP ownership explicitly — don't assume it's "obvious" who owns the work
5. Keep language as plain as possible — clauses people don't understand are clauses people don't honor
6. Get it reviewed before high-stakes use — templates are a strong starting point, not a substitute for legal review on big deals
